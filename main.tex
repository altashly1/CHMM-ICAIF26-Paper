\begin{abstract}
Low-state-count hidden Markov models (HMMs) with Gaussian emissions were long held unable to reproduce the slow autocorrelation decay of absolute daily equity returns. We separate that limitation into a temporal channel (the decay modes of the latent regime chain) and a distributional channel (the per-regime densities), comparing Gaussian, Student-$t$, Laplace, and generalized-error emissions under one forward--backward framework with family-specific generalized-ECM updates. A spectral identity bounds the number of geometric decay modes of the absolute-return autocorrelation by the rank of the centred transition matrix. On SPY a single persistent mode carries $96.8\%$ of the total absolute lag-1 modal contribution at the selected three-state fit, so marginal flexibility, not additional decay modes, explains more of the remaining fit gap there; on a 30-ticker panel (diagnostic at $K = 18$) the median dominant share is $0.73$, with a median of seven eigen-contributions needed for $95\%$, so the near-one-mode reading is SPY-specific rather than panel-wide. Evaluating one configuration at a time: a 12-parameter Gaussian specification captures the three symmetric stylized-fact diagnostics and supports a filter-conditional Value-at-Risk---the quantile of the one-step-ahead state-mixture predictive---that is not rejected by conditional-coverage backtests on held-out data or most walk-forward folds. A 15-parameter shared-$\nu$ Student-$t$ specification narrows the remaining excess-kurtosis gap without a penalty hyperparameter, and a penalised Student-$t$ variant fitted per ticker improves marginal fidelity on the non-stress cross-ticker panel under quarterly refitting. An i.i.d.\ bootstrap and a hidden semi-Markov benchmark with a moment-updated duration law match or exceed these models on single-window marginal fit; an analogous HSMM risk head is not evaluated here. The scope is daily US equities in stable out-of-sample periods; quarterly refitting improves marginal fidelity there but does not repair abrupt stress-regime shifts.
\end{abstract}
